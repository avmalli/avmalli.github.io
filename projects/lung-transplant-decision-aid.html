Lung Transplant Decision Aid

Human-computer interaction capstone project designing a clinical decision support tool to help patients understand transplant tradeoffs and prepare for evaluation.

Context

Patients with severe COPD often face a difficult choice: pursue lung transplantation or continue medical management. The survival benefit may be unclear for some patients, but the tradeoffs in risk, lifestyle, and long-term outcomes are substantial.

Working with a pulmonologist and researcher at UPMC, we designed a web-based decision aid intended to be used before evaluation week to improve comprehension, reduce overwhelm, and support values-aligned decisions.

Goal

  • Make the decision space explicit (patients have options, not a default path).
  • Communicate risks and tradeoffs clearly for varying health literacy.
  • Support reflection on personal values (quality of life, risk tolerance, priorities).
  • Prepare patients for the logistics and reality of evaluation and recovery.

My role

Technical lead on the team. I helped translate clinical outcomes into interpretable representations, shaped the decision framework, and iterated on interaction design to balance clarity, accuracy, and usability.

Approach

We combined stakeholder and user research with iterative prototyping:

  • Stakeholder interviews (clinicians, coordinators, support staff)
  • Review of existing decision aids and decision-science literature
  • Low → mid → high-fidelity prototypes
  • Usability testing focused on comprehension, navigation, and cognitive load

Key design decisions

  • Minimal information per screen to reduce cognitive load.
  • Guided flow + progress cues to keep patients oriented.
  • Risk visuals that are concrete (human figures rather than abstract dot plots).
  • Values prompts to help patients articulate what matters to them before appointments.
  • Patient narratives (where appropriate) because patients engaged more with peers than with physician explanations alone.

Why this is relevant beyond healthcare

This project is an example of designing for decision-making under uncertainty: translating complex, probabilistic information into interfaces that support reasoning under real constraints (stress, limited time, varying literacy, and high stakes).

It connects directly to broader questions I work on: how people interpret noisy information, how presentation changes decisions, and how to build tools that produce clearer, more defensible conclusions from messy real-world data.
