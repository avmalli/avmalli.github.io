Lung Transplant Decision Aid

A human-computer interaction capstone project focused on designing a clinical decision support tool for patients with severe COPD considering lung transplantation.

Problem

Patients with severe COPD often face a difficult choice: pursue lung transplantation or continue medical management. While survival rates can be similar across treatments, the tradeoffs in risk, quality of life, and complications are substantial.

Our client, a practicing pulmonologist and researcher at UPMC, observed that many patients misunderstood the risks, overestimated benefits, or felt overwhelmed during evaluation week. Many patients had low health literacy, limited computer experience, and high cognitive load.

Goal

Design a web-based decision aid that:

  • Clarifies that patients have a meaningful choice
  • Communicates risks and tradeoffs in digestible ways
  • Helps patients reflect on personal values
  • Improves comprehension before formal evaluation

My Role

I served as Technical Lead. I helped structure the decision framework, translate clinical data into interpretable representations, and design interactive components that balanced usability with statistical clarity.

Research & Design Process

User Research

  • Stakeholder interviews (pulmonologists, surgeons, coordinators, social workers)
  • Observation of patient counseling sessions
  • Client data analysis (COBALT dataset, DECIDE survey, focus group)
  • Review of decision science literature and existing decision aids

We identified a core challenge: patients often defaulted toward transplant without fully understanding complications, timelines, or long-term tradeoffs.

Iterative Prototyping

We developed low-, medium-, and high-fidelity prototypes, testing navigation structure, risk visualization, and values-elicitation quizzes.

  • Side-by-side comparison of transplant vs. medical management
  • Interactive values quizzes
  • Risk visualization using human figures rather than abstract dots
  • Progress-based guided navigation

Key Findings

  • Patients preferred patient video narratives over physician explanations.
  • Minimal information per screen improved comprehension.
  • Interactive value reflection increased engagement.
  • Risk statistics required careful wording and contextualization.
  • Navigation structure significantly impacted understanding.

Technical & Conceptual Contributions

This project sits at the intersection of behavioral science and decision support systems. It required translating probabilistic medical outcomes into formats that were accurate, interpretable, and usable under real cognitive constraints.

It reflects broader themes in my work: how people interpret uncertain information, how presentation affects decision quality, and how to design systems that support reasoning rather than overwhelm it.

Impact

The final high-fidelity prototype provided structured guidance through treatment tradeoffs and laid groundwork for continued clinical implementation and iPad-based testing.
